Major Research Project — MSc Trent University
AI Research  ·  Game Development  ·  Data Science  ·  State Space Analysis
Sokoban State Space Analysis: AI Solver Performance & Complexity
A full-stack AI research system built in Godot 4 with a Python solver backend, examining how puzzle complexity truly emerges — not from object counts, but from solver behaviour, duplication depth, and board geometry. Two search strategies were pitted against 35 custom levels across 22 AI sessions, producing a structured dataset of over 18,000 logged events. OLS regression showed block & button counts explain only 4% of effort variance. The hardest level, B011, took 3,135 solver actions — on a board with just 1 block and 1 button.

9Parallel Threads
8Puzzle Mechanics
4%Variance Explained by Object Count
  • Designed 8 novel puzzle mechanics including block duplication, floor-fill, hole-blocks, charged pushes, and switchables — each producing distinct state-space topologies that challenge traditional heuristic solvers in fundamentally different ways.
  • Implemented two AI search strategies: Beam Search (shortlist sizes 10–100, with dynamic restarts when exhausted) for standard block-button worlds; Parallel Priority Queue with 9 threads, shared 10M-state budget, and best-partial-path save for unsolved levels in floor-fill and hole-block worlds.
  • Built a GDScript DataLogger singleton persisting gameplay across scenes into 5 structured CSV tables (sessions, levels, objects, events, dup_events) — forming a reproducible research-grade dataset across human and AI play paths.
  • Conducted Kruskal-Wallis tests confirming statistically distinct effort distributions across design series (p=0.025), and Chi-square analysis proving duplication depth preference is genuine and non-random (p<0.0001) — deeper-generation duplicates consistently land in tighter positions with fewer valid cells.
  • Key finding: solver behaviour signals — action mix, RESET rate, and branching delta — predicted puzzle difficulty far more reliably than any structural level property, challenging the conventional assumption that object count drives complexity.
